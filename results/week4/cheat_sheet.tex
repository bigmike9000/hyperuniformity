Anticipated questions and answers.

\section{Why $\alpha$ can't exceed 3}

\Q{Is this a proof?}
Yes, for $|\det M|=1$ matrices. The bound $\alpha \leq 1 + 2/(r-1)$
follows from the determinant constraint and Pisot property.
The all-real case ($\alpha = 3$ for rank 2) is confirmed numerically
but not proven to be tight analytically.

\Q{Does this work for $|\det M| > 1$?}
The bound generalizes:
$\alpha \leq 1 + \frac{2}{r-1}(1 - \ln|\det M|/\ln\lambda_1)$.
For $|\det M| = 2$, rank 2: $\alpha < 3$ always.

\Q{Could any 1D substitution tiling have $\alpha > 3$?}
Not with $|\det M| = 1$ (proven). With $|\det M| > 1$, the bound is lower.
To get $\alpha > 3$ you would need $|\det M| < 1$,
which is not possible for non-negative integer matrices.

\section{$\bar\Lambda$ ranking}

\Q{Is there a formula for $\bar\Lambda$ of substitution tilings?}
No general formula. For URL: $\bar\Lambda = (1+a^2)/6$ (exact).
For stealthy: $\bar\Lambda \approx 1/(\pi^2\chi)$.
For substitutions: computed numerically.

\Q{Why is Fibonacci so close to the lattice?}
Fibonacci has balanced tile lengths ($L/S = \varphi \approx 1.618$),
so the spacing variation is modest.

\section{Metallic-mean $\bar\Lambda$ limit}

\Q{What do the n=200 and n=500 results show?}
$n = 200$: $\bar\Lambda = 0.334 \pm 0.022$.
$n = 500$: $\bar\Lambda = 0.324 \pm 0.027$.
Both consistent with $1/3$ but with large error bars.
The $n = 500$ value is actually below $1/3$ --- the error bars
are so large that the data cannot distinguish the limit from $1/3$.

\Q{Could the limit be exactly $1/3$?}
The data is consistent with it. $n = 1\ldots50$ approaches $1/3$
cleanly from below. The large-$n$ values scatter around $1/3$
rather than systematically increasing past it.

\Q{Why are large-$n$ measurements noisier?}
At $n = 500$, the chain grows by a factor of $\sim\!500$ per iteration.
Three iterations give 125M tiles, but only 3 ``generations'' of
quasi-periodic structure. The variance estimator needs many
oscillation periods to converge, and large-$n$ chains have very few.

\section{Yuan-Torquato improved spreadability}

\Q{Why does this help for BT but not Fibonacci at high order?}
BT: $\alpha = 1.545$ (non-integer), the asymptotic expansion
converges at order 5--6.
Fibonacci: $\alpha = 3$ (integer), dense Bragg peaks cause
the expansion to diverge past order 1.

\Q{Is this better than variance-based $\bar\Lambda$?}
No. Variance converges at $N \sim 10^3$ with no fitting.
Yuan method is useful when you only have spreadability data
(e.g.\ NMR experiments) and need $\alpha$.

\section{$|\det M|{=}2$ chains}

\Q{Why 138 instead of 28?}
28 was from row sums $\leq 6$. Expanding to row sums $\leq 10$
found many more matrices.

\Q{Why does $\bar\Lambda$ vary so much within each $\alpha$ group?}
Matrices with the same eigenvalues can have
very different substitution rules, producing different tile
arrangements. $\alpha$ captures only the leading spectral behavior;
$\bar\Lambda$ is sensitive to the full spatial structure.

\Q{Could we get $\alpha$ closer to 3?}
With $|\det M| = 2$: $\alpha = 3 - 2\ln 2/\ln\lambda_1$.
Need $\lambda_1 \to \infty$, meaning enormous row sums.
At $\lambda_1 = 100$: $\alpha = 2.70$. Slow approach.
