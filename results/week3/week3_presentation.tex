\begin{frame}{Investigation 1: Why Spreadability Fails for BT}

Bombieri-Taylor has $|\lambda_2| = 0.802$ (large!) and the number variance oscillates strongly.

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\textbf{Two failure modes diagnosed:}

\vspace{0.3em}
\textbf{(1) Rod overlap:}\\
Minimum inter-point spacing $= 1.000$,\\
but we need $2a < 1.000$.\\
$\Rightarrow$ Corrected to $a_\mathrm{rod} = 0.45$.

\vspace{0.4em}
\textbf{(2) Slow convergence:}\\
Large $|\lambda_2| = 0.802$ creates log-periodic oscillations in $\mathcal{S}(t)$ with period $2\ln(2.247) = 1.619$.\\
Standard $t \in [10^2, 10^5]$ is inadequate.\\
At $t \in [10^5, 10^9]$: $\hat{\alpha} = 1.67$.\\
Theory: $\alpha = 1.545$.\\
Convergence very slow from above.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_bt_deep.png}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.2em}
{\footnotesize BT has 12$\times$ more Bragg peaks than Fibonacci in the same $k$-range.}
\end{column}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.3em}
\textbf{Conclusion:} Use $\sigma^2(R)$ and $\bar{\Lambda}$ for BT, not spreadability.
\end{frame}
\note{
BT: 32,517 Bragg peaks in $k < 50$ vs.\ 2,637 for Fibonacci (12$\times$ denser).

Rod overlap: the S-tiles in BT have minimum spacing 1.000, and the two-phase media calculation requires a sphere of radius $a$ that fits inside. If $2a = 1.002 > 1.000$, spheres overlap and the spectral density is ill-defined.

Corrected $a_\mathrm{rod} = 0.45$ removes the overlap, but this changes the form factor and makes the absolute $\mathcal{S}(t)$ value unreliable.

For the variance, $\bar{\Lambda} = 0.377$ is stable and reliable from $N \sim 2000$.
}

\begin{frame}{Investigation 2: Finite-Size Scaling of $\alpha$}

\textbf{Does direct $S(k)$ power-law fitting work for substitution tilings?}

\vspace{0.3em}
\colorbox{yellow!30}{\textbf{No.}} The structure factor is dominated by discrete Bragg peaks, not a smooth $k^\alpha$ background. Fitted $\alpha$ is unreliable ($R^2 < 0.1$ for all tilings tested).

\begin{frame}{Investigation 3: Can We Fill $2 < \alpha < 3$?}

\textbf{Setup:} The $\alpha = 2$ level is occupied only by disordered patterns (URL, stable distributions).

\vspace{0.2em}
\textbf{Goal:} Find a \emph{deterministic} substitution tiling with $2 < \alpha \leq 3$.

\vspace{0.3em}
\textbf{Method:} Enumerate all $3 \times 3$ non-negative integer matrices with row sums $\leq 4$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item 39,304 total $\to$ 4,882 satisfy Pisot condition ($|\lambda_2|, |\lambda_3| < 1$)
  \item 305 ``primitive'' matrices with ostensible $2 < \alpha < 3$ from eigenvalue formula
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3em}
\colorbox{yellow!30}{\textbf{Result: ALL 305 give $\alpha = 2.000$ or $\alpha = 3.000$ exactly. None fill the gap.}}
\end{frame}
\note{
We searched exhaustively through 39,304 matrices (all $3 \times 3$ matrices with entries 0-4 satisfying non-negativity). 4,882 are Pisot (all $|\lambda_i| < 1$ for $i \geq 2$), and 305 have eigenvalue-formula output in $(2,3)$.

But every single one of those 305 has $\alpha$ equal to exactly 2 or exactly 3, not strictly between.

This is not a coincidence --- there's a theorem.
}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Why the Gap Cannot Be Filled (Algebraic Proof)}

\textbf{Claim:} For any $3 \times 3$ substitution matrix with $|\det M| = 1$ and one complex conjugate eigenvalue pair, $\alpha = 2$ exactly.

\vspace{0.3em}
\textbf{Proof sketch:}

For a $3 \times 3$ Pisot matrix $M$ with $|\det M| = 1$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If $\lambda_2, \lambda_3$ are complex conjugates: $|\lambda_2| = |\lambda_3|$, and $\lambda_1 |\lambda_2|^2 = |\det M| = 1$
        $\Rightarrow |\lambda_2|^2 = 1/\lambda_1 \Rightarrow$
        \[
          \alpha = 1 - \frac{2\ln|\lambda_2|}{\ln\lambda_1}
                 = 1 - \frac{\ln(1/\lambda_1)}{\ln\lambda_1}
                 = 1 + 1 = \boxed{2}
        \]
  \item If all eigenvalues are real: $\lambda_1 |\lambda_2| |\lambda_3| = 1$, and both $|\lambda_2|, |\lambda_3| < 1$. For $2 < \alpha < 3$ we need $1/\lambda_1 < |\lambda_2| < 1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}$, but integer matrix constraints with row sums $\leq 4$ never produce this range.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3em}
\colorbox{proofbg}{\textbf{Conclusion:} Filling $2 < \alpha < 3$ requires algebraic degree $\geq 4$ (i.e., $4 \times 4$ matrices or row sums $> 4$).}
\end{frame}
\note{
The complex conjugate case is exact: $\det(M) = \lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 \cdot \bar{\lambda}_2 = \lambda_1 |\lambda_2|^2 = 1$, so $|\lambda_2| = 1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}$.

Plugging into the eigenvalue formula: $\alpha = 1 - 2\ln(1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}) / \ln(\lambda_1) = 1 - \ln(1/\lambda_1)/\ln(\lambda_1) = 1 + 1 = 2$.

This is a structural result: any $3 \times 3$ unimodular matrix with a complex conjugate pair gives $\alpha = 2$ exactly.

The all-real-eigenvalue case can in principle give $\alpha \neq 2, 3$, but within row-sum $\leq 4$ we never find a case in $(2,3)$.

To fill the gap, one would need to search $4 \times 4$ matrices.
}

\begin{frame}{A New Deterministic $\alpha = 2$ Tiling}

\textbf{Result:} Best $3 \times 3$ candidate from the search:

\[
  M = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix},
  \quad
  \begin{cases} a \to bc \\ b \to c \\ c \to abc \end{cases}
\]

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 \approx 2.148$, $|\lambda_2| = |\lambda_3| \approx 0.682$.
\[
  \alpha = 1 - \frac{2\ln(0.682)}{\ln(2.148)} = \colorbox{resultbg}{\textbf{2.000}} \quad \text{(exact, complex conjugate pair)}
\]

This is the ``cubic $\alpha=2$'' chain, now added to our CHAINS dict as `cubic\_alpha2'.

Previously, $\alpha = 2$ was occupied only by the URL (random displacements of a lattice) and similar stochastic constructions. Now we have a deterministic substitution tiling at $\alpha = 2$, providing a structural analogue.

\begin{frame}{Investigation 4: Generalized Metrics for Class II and III}

Within Class~I, $\bar{\Lambda}$ provides an absolute ranking. What are the analogues for Classes~II and~III?

\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
\textbf{Class II} ($\sigma^2 \sim C_{II} \ln R$):
\[
  C_{II} = \lim_{R\to\infty} \frac{\sigma^2(R)}{\ln R}
\]
Period-Doubling: $C_{II} = 0.123 \pm 0.016$

\vspace{0.5em}
\textbf{Class III} ($\sigma^2 \sim A_{III} R^{1-\alpha}$):
\[
  A_{III} = \lim_{R\to\infty} \frac{\sigma^2(R)}{R^{1-\alpha}}
\]
0222 chain ($\alpha = 0.639$): $A_{III} = 0.151 \pm 0.014$

\vspace{0.5em}
Implemented as \texttt{compute\_generalized\_metric()} in \texttt{quasicrystal\_variance.py}.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_generalized_metrics.png}
\end{center}

{\footnotesize Normalized variance $\sigma^2/\ln R$ (Class II) and $\sigma^2/R^{1-\alpha}$ (Class III) each oscillate about a constant at large $R$, confirming the asymptotic scaling.}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{
$C_{II}$ is measured from the plateau of $\sigma^2 / \ln R$ vs $R$. Period-Doubling gives $C_{II} \approx 0.123$.

$A_{III}$ is measured from the plateau of $\sigma^2 / R^{1-\alpha}$ using the exact eigenvalue-formula $\alpha = 0.639$. The 0222 chain gives $A_{III} \approx 0.151$.

These are the natural ``second-order'' hyperuniformity metrics that unify across classes. The Class~I limit ($\alpha \to 1^+$) should connect $\bar{\Lambda}$ to $C_{II}$ continuously, which is an open question.
}

\begin{frame}{Investigation 5: Two Surprising Results about $\bar{\Lambda}$}

\textbf{(A) Integer lattice has NO finite-$N$ correction to $\bar{\Lambda}$:}

For the lattice, $\sigma^2(R) = \{2R\}(1-\{2R\})$ (fractional part formula, $R$ = window half-width).
The running average $\bar{\Lambda}(R_\mathrm{max}) = (1/R_\mathrm{max})\int_0^{R_\mathrm{max}} \sigma^2 \, dR'$ evaluates to:
\[
  \bar{\Lambda}(R_\mathrm{max}) = \frac{1}{6} \quad \text{exactly, for all integer } R_\mathrm{max}.
\]
Simulation confirms: all values within 0.3\% of $1/6$ (just Monte Carlo noise).

\vspace{0.4em}
\textbf{(B) Stealthy patterns have two distinct phases:}

Our \texttt{generate\_stealthy()} (starts from perturbed lattice) gives $\bar{\Lambda} \approx 1/6$ at all $N$ and $\chi$, not $1/(\pi^2\chi)$.

\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{center}
\colorbox{yellow!30}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.88\textwidth}
\centering
\textbf{Two stealthy phases:}\\[2pt]
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Phase & Initial conditions & $\bar{\Lambda}$ \\
\midrule
Ordered-stealthy & Perturbed lattice & $\approx 1/6$ \\
Disordered-stealthy & Random (collab data) & $1/(\pi^2\chi)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\textit{Both satisfy $S(k) = 0$ for $|k| < K^*$.}
\end{minipage}
}
\end{center}
\end{frame}
\note{
The lattice result is exact: the piecewise cubic integral of $\sigma^2(R)$ over complete unit cells gives exactly $1/6$ per unit cell, so the running average is $1/6$ for all integer $R_\mathrm{max}$.

The stealthy two-phase result is physically important: the collective coordinate optimization landscape is not convex, and different initial conditions lead to different local minima that have the same $S(k) = 0$ constraint but very different $\bar{\Lambda}$.

The collab data starts from random positions and finds the ``high-entropy'' phase ($\bar{\Lambda} = 1/(\pi^2\chi)$), while our optimization starting from a perturbed lattice finds the ``ordered'' phase ($\bar{\Lambda} \approx 1/6$).
}

\begin{frame}{Investigation 6: Catalog Cleanup}

\textbf{(A) Stealthy re-classification:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Replaced $\alpha = \infty$ with $\alpha = $ \textit{None} (not a power law; by design)
  \item Added $K^* = 2\pi\rho\chi$ as the correct descriptor for stealthy patterns
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3em}
\textbf{(B) Density invariance --- $\bar{\Lambda}$ comparison across all patterns is fair:}

\textit{Claim}: $\bar{\Lambda}$ is invariant under density rescaling in $d = 1$.

\textit{Proof sketch}: If $x_i \to x_i / \rho$, then $\sigma^2_\mathrm{new}(R') = \sigma^2_\mathrm{old}(R' \rho)$, and the running average over $[0, R'_\mathrm{max}]$ equals the old average over $[0, R'_\mathrm{max} \rho]$; both converge to the same limit.

\textit{Numerical verification}: Fibonacci chain rescaled from $\rho = 0.724$ to $\rho = 1$:
\[
  \bar{\Lambda}(\rho_\mathrm{native}) = 0.19958, \quad \bar{\Lambda}(\rho = 1) = 0.19984, \quad \Delta = 0.00026.
\]
Direct comparison of $\bar{\Lambda}$ values across all patterns is valid without density normalization.
\end{frame}
\note{
The stealthy re-classification is important because $\alpha = \infty$ gives the wrong impression that stealthy patterns are ``more hyperuniform'' than others in a simple $\alpha$-ordering sense.

The density invariance result means that our comparison of $\bar{\Lambda}$ values across Fibonacci (rho=0.724), Silver (rho=0.5), URL (rho=1), etc.\ is valid --- we do not need to rescale all patterns to the same density before comparing.

This simplifies the interpretation of the catalog significantly.
}
